ORIENTATION & PPE
General safety orientation for all Atlas Home Pro field and office personnel. Required before any job site activity.

Core Hazards
🔥
HEAT & BURNS

⚡
ELECTRICAL

🧪

Core Module 02
INCIDENT PROTOCOL
What to do when something goes wrong. Every Atlas employee must know these steps cold before their first job.
Required — All Staff
Immediate Response — Any Injury or Emergency
1
Stop Work Immediately

Cease all activity. Secure the area. Do not move the injured person unless there is immediate danger (fire, electrocution, structural collapse).

2
Call 911 If Needed

Any broken bone, laceration requiring stitches, loss of consciousness, electrical shock, or fall from height = call 911. Don't second-guess. Do it.

3
Call Supervisor Within 15 Minutes

Atlas supervisor must be notified of any incident — even near-misses — within 15 minutes. No exceptions. This is non-negotiable.

4
Document & Photograph

Take photos of the scene before anything is moved. Note time, conditions, what task was being performed, PPE in use, and any witnesses.

5
Complete Written Incident Report — Same Day

Submit the Atlas Incident Report Form (in HouseCall Pro job notes) before end of shift. All information must be factual — no speculation.


⚠ Never Do These Things
  • Do not admit fault, apologize, or speculate about cause to the customer
  • Do not delete photos or discuss the incident on social media
  • Do not return to work on the same job until cleared by supervisor
  • Do not skip reporting because the injury "seems minor" — near-misses count
Property Damage Protocol
If You Damage Customer Property
  • Acknowledge the damage calmly and professionally to the customer
  • Call supervisor immediately — do not agree to personal repair or offer cash
  • Document damage with photos in HouseCall Pro job notes
  • Atlas insurance covers accidental damage — follow the claims process

Core Module 03
FLEET & DRIVING
Atlas vans are moving billboards and job sites on wheels. Driving standards protect the brand, the team, and the public.
Required — All Drivers
Before Every Drive
  • Walk-around check: tires, lights, mirrors, fuel
  • All cargo properly secured in van
  • Tools stowed — nothing loose in cabin
  • Phone on Do Not Disturb (calls to Bluetooth only)
  • Valid CA license and proof of insurance in vehicle
Zero Tolerance
  • No texting/handheld phone while driving
  • No speeding in residential zones (>5mph over)
  • No driving after alcohol or impairment
  • No unauthorized passengers
  • No personal use of Atlas vans
Parking & Customer Property
Parking Standards
  • Never block driveways, fire hydrants, or handicap spaces
  • Park on street when possible — only in driveway with customer permission
  • Use cones/safety markers when working near the vehicle on the street
  • Leave 3 feet of clearance when backing into tight spots — use spotter

Trade Module 01
PLUMBING SAFETY
Plumbers work in wet, confined, and chemically active environments. Know your hazards before you pick up the wrench.
Plumbing Techs & Supervisors
Top Plumbing Hazards
  • Burns from pressurized hot water and steam lines (drain before opening)
  • Electric shock in wet environments — use GFCI tools only
  • Asbestos and lead pipe in pre-1980 LA homes — test before cutting
  • Sewer gas (H2S) in drain work — ventilate, never enter unventilated crawl spaces alone
  • Heavy pipe lifting — team-lift cast iron 4"+ always
PPE — Plumbing
  • Face shield when opening pressurized lines
  • Chemical-resistant gloves for drain work
  • Knee pads for under-sink / floor work
  • GFCI extension cords — required in all wet environments
  • N95 respirator in older homes (pre-1980)
Before Every Plumbing Job
  • Locate and confirm the water shutoff with customer
  • Test for lead and asbestos on older pipe (note job card)
  • Verify no electrical lines share the work wall
  • Place absorbent mats/tarps before any drain opening
  • Communicate timeline clearly — no surprises

Trade Module 02
HVAC SAFETY
Refrigerants, high voltage, rooftop access, and confined attic spaces make HVAC one of the higher-risk trades.
HVAC Techs & Supervisors
Top HVAC Hazards
  • High-voltage capacitors — must discharge before servicing (stored energy can kill after power-off)
  • Refrigerant exposure — frostbite, asphyxiation in enclosed spaces, EPA regulations
  • Rooftop falls — 4ft+ requires fall protection equipment
  • Attic heat illness — attics reach 150°F in LA summers; 20-min max rotations
  • Carbon monoxide from combustion equipment — CO detector required on all furnace jobs
EPA 608 Requirements
  • All HVAC technicians must hold EPA 608 certification
  • Refrigerant must never be vented — recovery equipment required
  • Refrigerant cylinders stored upright in ventilated van area
  • Report any refrigerant release to supervisor immediately
Lockout / Tagout (LOTO)
  • Disconnect and lock ALL power before internal unit service
  • Discharge capacitors with insulated tool before touching
  • Tag equipment with your name, date, and "DO NOT ENERGIZE"
  • Verify zero energy state with meter before proceeding

Trade Module 03
ELECTRICAL SAFETY
Electricity kills fast and silently. Atlas electricians follow arc flash, LOTO, and grounding protocols on every single job — no exceptions.
Electrical Techs & Supervisors
Top Electrical Hazards
  • Electrocution — always assume circuits are live until proven otherwise
  • Arc flash / arc blast — can reach 35,000°F; proper PPE category required
  • Improper grounding on older LA homes (knob-and-tube, aluminum wiring)
  • Working in wet locations without GFCI protection
  • Overloaded circuits — test load before adding circuits
The 6 Electrical Safety Rules — Atlas Non-Negotiables
  • Never work on live circuits unless voltage testing requires it — and only with Category-rated PPE
  • LOTO before opening any panel: lock, verify with meter, tag with name
  • Test every wire with a non-contact tester before touching
  • GFCI required on all outdoor, garage, kitchen, and bathroom circuits
  • Call out old wiring to homeowner — document, photograph, get sign-off
  • Two-person rule for panel work over 240V

Trade Module 04
WATER RESTORATION
Water damage jobs involve mold, biohazards, structural instability, and hidden electrical risks. IICRC standards are the baseline.
Restoration Techs & Supervisors
Water Restoration Hazards
  • Category 2/3 water (gray/black) — biohazard, hepatitis, E. coli risk
  • Mold spore inhalation — respiratory damage, liability if not disclosed
  • Structural instability — wet drywall, subfloor, ceiling can collapse
  • Electrical in flooded areas — DO NOT ENTER until power is confirmed off
  • Asbestos in older homes — disturbing wet drywall/flooring can release fibers
Water Categories — Know the Difference
  • Cat 1 — Clean: Supply line, tub overflow. Standard PPE.
  • Cat 2 — Gray: Dishwasher, washing machine. Chemical PPE + gloves.
  • Cat 3 — Black: Sewage, flooding. Full PPE, Tyvek suit, N95/P100.
IICRC Standards — Atlas Minimum
  • All restoration techs must hold IICRC WRT certification
  • Moisture readings documented before and after on every job
  • Containment barriers required for any mold-suspected work
  • Air movers and dehumidifiers set per IICRC S500 drying protocol

Trade Module 06
FINISHING & RESTORATION
Painting, drywall, and patching involve chemical exposure, silica dust, and working at heights. Know the SDS for every product you use.
Finishing Techs & Supervisors
Finishing & Painting Hazards
  • VOC exposure from paints, solvents, primers — ventilation mandatory, no open flames
  • Silica dust from drywall sanding — long-term lung disease risk (silicosis)
  • Lead paint in pre-1978 homes — EPA RRP rule applies, no sanding without testing
  • Ladder/scaffold falls — same 6ft height rules apply
  • Eye and skin burns from chemical strippers and caustic agents
Lead Paint — EPA RRP Rule
  • Any home built before 1978 must be assumed to have lead paint
  • Atlas requires RRP-certified tech or supervisor on all pre-1978 jobs
  • Wet sanding, HEPA vacuum, and containment required before disturbance
  • Notify customer in writing — EPA Renovate Right pamphlet required
PPE — Finishing
  • Half-face respirator with P100 + OV cartridges for paint spraying
  • N95 minimum for drywall sanding and dry cutting
  • Chemical splash goggles for strippers and caustic agents
  • Nitrile gloves for all chemical and paint work

Trade Module 07
GATES & WELDING
Welding and metal fabrication involve UV radiation, fire hazards, fumes, and high-voltage equipment. Never weld without a proper fire watch.
Welding Techs & Supervisors
Welding & Gate Hazards
  • UV/IR radiation — arc eye (photokeratitis) from unshielded arc; auto-darkening helmet required
  • Fumes — metal fume fever from zinc, lead, and coated metals; outdoor or ventilated work only
  • Fire hazard — hot work within 35ft of combustibles requires fire watch and permit
  • Electric shock from welding equipment — especially in wet conditions
  • Heavy gate/panel handling — crush injuries; use dolly, never solo-lift gates
Hot Work Requirements
  • Verbal hot work approval from supervisor before any welding on customer property
  • Fire extinguisher (ABC) within 10ft — checked before work starts
  • Remove or wet-down combustibles within 35ft of weld area
  • Fire watch must remain on-site 30 minutes after welding stops
PPE — Welding
  • Auto-darkening welding helmet (shade 10-13 for MIG/TIG/Stick)
  • Leather welding gloves and leather apron
  • P100 + OV respirator for coated or galvanized metals
  • Leather boots (not rubber — to ground static)
  • Cotton/fire-resistant clothing — no synthetics near arc

Weekly Toolbox Talk — Supervisor Guide

Run a 10-minute safety huddle every Monday morning. Rotate topics weekly. Document attendance.

📅
WEEK 1 — PPE

Go around the team: what PPE do you have? Is anything damaged? Do a show-your-boots/glasses check. Replace anything that fails.

📅
WEEK 2 — NEAR MISSES

Ask: "Did anyone see anything close this week?" No blame. Normalize reporting. Discuss one real near-miss from the field.

📅
WEEK 3 — TRADE HAZARD

Pick one trade-specific hazard (rotating through all 7 trades). 5-minute video + 5-minute discussion. Use YouTube search links above.

📅
WEEK 4 — INCIDENT REVIEW

Review any incidents or customer complaints from the month. What happened, what changed. No blame — system improvement focus.

Toolbox Talk Tips for Supervisors
  • Keep it to 10 minutes — more than that loses the room
  • Ask questions, don't lecture — "What would you do if..." format works best
  • Document attendance in HouseCall Pro job notes or a shared sheet
  • Reward participation — a simple "good point" goes a long way
  • Bilingual delivery matters — have Spanish-speaking leads run Spanish sessions
Incident Investigation — Supervisor Protocol

Required for any injury, near-miss, or property damage. Complete within 24 hours.

1
Secure Scene & Ensure Safety

Confirm no ongoing hazard. Arrange medical care if needed. Do not disturb the scene before documenting.

2
Gather Evidence — First Hour

Photographs from multiple angles. Identify all witnesses. Collect tool/equipment involved. Note weather, lighting, and conditions.

3
Interview Separately — No Groupthink

Interview the tech and any witnesses separately before they talk to each other. Ask open-ended questions: "Walk me through exactly what you were doing..."

4
Root Cause Analysis — Not Blame

Use "5 Whys": why did it happen? Why did that happen? Keep asking until you reach a systemic root cause (training gap, equipment failure, unclear procedure).

5
Corrective Action Plan

What changes to prevent recurrence? Update SOPs, add training, replace equipment, change procedures. Assign owner and deadline. Document in HouseCall Pro.

6
Share Learnings (Anonymized) at Next Toolbox Talk

Share what happened and what changed — without naming the tech. This builds safety culture faster than any training video.
