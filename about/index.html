§ About · The principal

Thirty years,
one city.

David Safai is a Los Angeles real estate operator, multifamily developer, and Class B licensed general contractor. Atlas Home Pro is his editorial platform, and Atlas Home Builders his building practice.

The two are the same job. Writing about a market you do not operate in is consulting. Operating in a market you do not write about is anonymous. Atlas Home Pro is the refusal to choose.

§ The long version

Owner, builder, writer — in that order.

I was not born into Los Angeles real estate. I arrived, like most of the people I respect in this business, by taking on one building I probably should not have bought, and then another, and then another, for thirty years. Along the way I bought a general contractor's license, because the only thing worse than a bad operator is an operator at the mercy of a bad contractor.

I have developed ground-up multifamily, including The Felix on Fairfax — forty-three rental units we still hold and operate — and Olympic Towers, twelve for-sale condominiums we delivered, sold, and continue to steward through the HOA. I have bought and repositioned smaller courtyard buildings across the basin. I have sat on both sides of every table in this business: the buyer, the seller, the landlord, the borrower, the builder, and the permit applicant.

Atlas Home Pro began as a set of internal memos — the kind an operator writes to a junior partner after a walk-through, or to a lender before a renewal call. At some point it became clear that the people I respect most in this business are the ones who read and write carefully about it. So we started publishing. Not to sell anything. To make the thinking visible.

The company is family-capitalized. We have no outside investors, no carried interest clock, and no reason to exit. If you have read this far and are wondering what that actually means, it means we can take the long view on a job, on a tenant, on a neighborhood, without apologizing for it to a fund-level IRR.

§ On how we work

Four principles, held honestly.

I.

Owner first, always.

The principal is not a name on the letterhead. He walks the job, takes the call, and signs the closeout. If you need a full-time CEO of your project, you have hired the wrong firm.

II.

Long-hold math.

Family-capitalized and proud of it. We underwrite buildings we would still want to own in 2045. That discipline leaks into everything: tenant selection, sequencing, material choice, warranty work.

III.

Self-perform where it matters.

Seven trades, one schedule. A GC who can open every wall and every panel is a GC who can tell you the truth about what is behind them. That is worth the overhead, in our experience.

IV.

Publish the thinking.

Atlas Brief is not marketing; it is the in-house memo turned outward. If we believe something strongly enough to bet a building on it, we write it down — with numbers — and put our name on it.
