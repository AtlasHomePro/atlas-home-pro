Electric Gate Installation Cost in Los Angeles — 2026 Published Pricing

What electric gate installation actually costs in Westside LA. Breakdown by gate type, motor, access control, and permit requirements. Published pricing — no surprise quotes.

Atlas Home Pro 9 min read Licensed & Insured
Licensed & Insured Permit Handling Included Upfront Pricing West LA Specialists

Installing an electric gate in Los Angeles involves more components — and more variables — than most homeowners expect when they start getting quotes. The fabricated gate itself is one cost. The motor operator is another. Electrical service to the gate post, access control, permits, and concrete or pillar work all add to the total. This guide breaks down what each component costs in the LA market in 2026, so you can build an accurate budget and evaluate quotes with confidence.

The Five Components of an Electric Gate System

An electric gate system is not a single purchase — it is a combination of five distinct components, each quoted and priced separately by most contractors. Understanding each one before you solicit bids prevents the most common frustration: a low opening quote that grows once the scope is clarified.

1. Gate fabrication and installation is the steel or iron structure itself, custom-fabricated to your opening width and designed to match your home's aesthetic. Cost range: $2,500–$25,000+ depending on material, size, and design complexity. A simple vertical picket swing gate for a standard 12-foot residential driveway sits at the lower end; a custom ornamental double-leaf gate for an estate entry can exceed this significantly.

2. The operator (motor) is the electromechanical device that opens and closes the gate. Installed cost: $800–$3,500 depending on gate type and weight class. Arm operators for swing gates, underground hydraulic systems, and rack-and-pinion drives for sliding gates each carry different price points.

3. Electrical service runs conduit from your main electrical panel to the gate pillar. This is the cost that catches first-time buyers off guard. Typical residential run: $600–$3,500. Hillside properties in Bel Air or Brentwood with long conduit runs can reach $4,500.

4. Access control covers how residents and guests operate the gate — from a basic keypad at $150–$400 up to a high-resolution video intercom with smartphone integration at $2,500–$6,000. This is the component with the widest range, and the right tier depends entirely on your usage and security requirements.

5. Permits and inspections are required for all new gate installations across Los Angeles County jurisdictions. Expect $150–$600 depending on the city. Atlas handles all permit applications as part of the installation contract.

When these five components are combined, total installed costs break into three tiers: basic ($5,500–$12,000), mid-range ($10,000–$25,000), and custom estate ($20,000–$65,000+).

Swing vs. Sliding Gates — The Decision That Drives Cost

The choice between a swing gate and a sliding gate is the single biggest engineering decision in the project — and it directly determines hardware costs, site prep requirements, and operator selection. Making this decision correctly at the outset prevents expensive mid-project changes.

Swing gates open on hinges mounted to posts or pillars, sweeping inward or outward in an arc. They carry lower hardware costs than sliding gates and can be configured as single leaf (one gate panel) or double leaf (two panels meeting at center). The critical constraint is clearance: the gate needs unobstructed arc space equal to its full width. A flat, level driveway with adequate setback from the street is the natural habitat for a swing gate. On short driveways, sloped approaches, or any lot where the gate arc would sweep into a parked vehicle or a landscaped edge, a swing gate creates operational problems.

Sliding gates travel laterally along the fence line, eliminating the arc constraint entirely. The hardware package is more complex — a motor unit, ground track, and chain or rack-and-pinion drive — which adds cost. But for hillside properties in Bel Air, Laurel Canyon, and the Brentwood hills, or for compact urban lots in West Hollywood and Century City where driveway depth is limited, sliding is often the only practical choice. The gate does require a clear run along the fence equal to the gate's full width plus a buffer — this rules out sliding gates when the fence line is interrupted by a pedestrian entry or utility panel.

Cantilever sliding gates eliminate the ground track entirely. The gate is suspended from rollers mounted to posts and cantilevered out over the opening. This configuration handles uneven or unpaved surfaces cleanly — useful on gravel driveways or properties where a ground track would be disrupted by drainage or seasonal ground movement. Cantilever gates carry the highest hardware cost in the sliding category, but they're the right solution when ground conditions rule out a standard track.

Gate Motor (Operator) Selection

The motor operator is the mechanical heart of the system. Under-specifying it is one of the most common installation mistakes — a motor working near its rated capacity fails early, particularly under the thermal cycling conditions of a Los Angeles summer. The rule of thumb: overspec by 30% of the gate's rated weight.

Electromechanical arm operators are the most common choice for residential swing gates. The arm mounts to the gate frame and extends to a bracket on the post. Residential-grade units run $400–$900 for the operator alone; installed cost including labor is typically $800–$1,600. These are reliable, serviceable, and widely supported. Brands like LiftMaster, FAAC, and BFT dominate the LA market.

Underground hydraulic operators mount below grade, concealing the mechanism entirely. The result is a clean aesthetic — no visible arm, no hardware on the gate face — that suits the architectural expectations of high-end properties in 90210 and 90077. The operator alone runs $1,200–$2,800; installed cost including the below-grade excavation and concrete work is $2,500–$5,000 per gate leaf. These systems require more skilled installation but carry a premium look that is effectively invisible once complete.

Linear actuators are compact, quiet arm operators that mount inside the gate frame. They're well-suited to lighter residential swing gates up to about 800 lbs. Operator cost: $300–$700.

For sliding gates, the two primary operator types are rack-and-pinion chain drives ($500–$1,200 for the motor unit) and belt drives ($700–$1,500). Belt drives run quieter — relevant for residential properties where the gate cycles near bedroom windows or late at night. Both require a gear rack welded to the bottom rail of the gate, adding $200–$400 to fabrication cost.

Electrical Service to the Gate — The Hidden Cost

Every electric gate requires 120V power at the gate pillar or post. If you don't already have an outlet within 20 feet of the gate location — and most properties don't — the electrical service run is a separate line item that needs to be scoped before you finalize your gate budget.

A typical residential electrical service run involves trenching, installing conduit, pulling wire from the main panel to the gate location, and installing a GFCI outlet or direct connection point at the pillar. For a standard flat lot with a 50- to 80-foot run from panel to gate, installed cost runs $600–$2,500. Longer runs on large Bel Air or hillside properties — where the panel is inside the home and the gate is at the street end of a 200-foot driveway — can reach $3,500–$4,500.

Properties where trenching is particularly disruptive or expensive (hardscape driveways, landscaped gardens, steep grades) sometimes opt for solar-powered operators. A solar kit — panel, charge controller, and battery bank mounted at the gate pillar — eliminates the conduit run entirely. Budget $800–$2,000 for a quality solar kit. It's a legitimate engineering choice when the trenching cost would otherwise be disproportionate, but it requires clear sun exposure at the gate location and adds a maintenance variable (battery replacement every 4–6 years).

Battery backup is included with most modern operator units. Verify this is the case with any operator you're specifying — it ensures the gate remains operational during power outages, which is a code and safety consideration for gates that control vehicle access.

Permit Requirements by Zip Code

Every Los Angeles-area jurisdiction requires permits for new gate installations. The specific requirements, fees, and review timelines vary by city. Plan for 2–4 weeks from permit submission to approval. Atlas handles all permit applications as part of the installation contract — this is not a separate service or add-on.

Beverly Hills (90210): Permit required for all new gate installations. The Beverly Hills Building and Safety Division reviews gate projects, and properties within HOA boundaries or historic overlay zones may require additional approvals. Contact BHPD for requirements specific to your property address.

Santa Monica (90401–90405): Permit required. Santa Monica's Building and Safety Division reviews all new automated gate installations for structural and electrical compliance.

West Hollywood (90046, 90069): Permit required. Setback rules apply — gates must be set back from the property line per WeHo municipal code. Given the compact lot sizes typical of 90046, this setback requirement directly affects gate design and operator selection.

Los Angeles City (90036, 90025, 90024, 90046, 90066, 90064): Permit required for structures and gates over 6 feet in height. Automated gates trigger electrical permit requirements in addition to the structural permit. The LA Department of Building and Safety (LADBS) manages these applications.

Culver City and Marina del Rey (90292): Permit required. Culver City has its own Building Division; Marina del Rey properties in unincorporated LA County fall under LA County Department of Public Works jurisdiction.

For zip codes 90049 (Brentwood), 90077 (Bel Air), and 90025 (West LA / Westwood): these are Los Angeles City jurisdiction and follow LADBS requirements, with potential HOA review layer for gated communities and hillside overlays.

Cost in Specific LA Zip Codes

Electric gate costs in Los Angeles vary not just by gate spec but by zip code — because property type, lot geometry, typical installation complexity, and HOA requirements all correlate with neighborhood. Here is what to budget by area:

90210 (Beverly Hills): Estate-grade installations are the norm. Typical project range: $20,000–$65,000+. HOA and city review is standard. Masonry pillar work, underground operators, and high-end video intercom systems are the baseline expectation. Budget conservatively — permit and design review timelines are longer than in other jurisdictions.

90077 (Bel Air): Hillside complexity adds cost. Engineered footings are often required on sloped sites; the electrical service run can be long. Typical range: $15,000–$50,000. Sliding and cantilever gate configurations are common here due to the prevalence of sloped driveways.

90049 (Brentwood): Mix of flat and hilly lots. Mid-range to custom scope. Typical range: $10,000–$30,000. Most projects involve a standard swing or sliding gate with arm operator and video intercom.

90046 (West Hollywood): Compact lots, modern architectural preferences. Typical range: $5,500–$18,000. Sliding gates are common given the lot geometry. Modern clean-line fabrication is the standard aesthetic choice.

90036 (Fairfax / Mid-City): Standard residential SFR installations. Typical range: $5,500–$15,000. Single and double swing gates with arm operators and keypad or basic intercom.

90024 and 90025 (Westwood / West LA): Varied — from apartment community motor upgrades to SFR custom installations. Typical range: $3,000–$20,000.

90064 (Rancho Park / West LA): Similar to 90025, primarily residential SFR scope. Range: $5,500–$18,000.

90066 (Mar Vista): 1950s SFR stock, often first-time gate installation on properties that have never had one. Typical range: $5,500–$15,000. Electrical service run is a significant variable here because these homes often have panels that require substantial conduit work to reach the street-facing gate location.

90292 (Marina del Rey): HOA common-gate upgrades and condominium entry systems are common in addition to individual property installations. Typical range: $4,000–$15,000. Projects often involve replacing or upgrading existing gate infrastructure rather than new installation from scratch.

A detailed quote should itemize gate fabrication, operator, electrical service run, access control, and permits as separate line items. If a quote bundles everything into a single number, ask for the breakdown before signing.

Questions to Ask Before Signing a Gate Installation Contract

Before committing to any gate contractor, these five questions separate thorough proposals from incomplete ones:

1. Is the motor included in the gate price, or quoted separately? Some fabricators quote the gate and operator together; others quote gate only. An all-in quote that looks competitive may not include the operator — confirm before comparing bids.

2. Is the electrical service run included? This is the most common scope gap. If the quote doesn't explicitly address the conduit run from panel to gate, ask what assumptions the contractor is making about existing electrical access at the gate location.

3. What is the motor brand, model, and weight rating? Ask specifically. The operator brand and weight class directly determine the system's reliability and service cost over time. An underspecified motor on a heavy gate will fail. Acceptable residential brands include LiftMaster, FAAC, BFT, Viking, and Elite. Require that the motor be sized at a minimum 30% above the gate's actual weight.

4. What happens if permits are delayed — is there a penalty clause? Permitting timelines are outside the contractor's control, but the contract should specify how delays are handled and confirm that your project is not back-scheduled without notice.

5. What is the warranty on fabrication vs. motor vs. access control? These are three separate warranty obligations, typically from three different parties. Fabrication warranties from the fabricator; operator warranties from the manufacturer (typically 1–5 years depending on brand); access control warranties from the intercom/access manufacturer. Confirm each in writing.
