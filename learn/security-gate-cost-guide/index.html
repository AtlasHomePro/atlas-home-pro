Custom Security Gate Costs in Los Angeles: 2026 Price Guide

Custom iron security gates in LA range from $3,500 for a basic pedestrian gate to $25,000+ for a fully automated estate entry. Here's what drives the price.

Atlas Home Pro 8 min read Licensed & Insured
Licensed & Insured Same-Day Available Upfront Pricing West LA Specialists

A custom security gate is one of the most impactful investments an LA homeowner can make — simultaneously improving curb appeal, property security, and home value. But pricing varies wildly depending on gate type, size, design complexity, automation, and finish. A basic swing gate for a pedestrian entry might cost $3,500 installed, while a fully automated sliding estate gate with intercom integration can exceed $25,000. This guide breaks down exactly what you're paying for.

Security Gate Pricing by Type

The type of gate you need is the single biggest cost variable. Most residential security gates in Los Angeles fall into five categories: pedestrian swing gates, single driveway swing gates, double driveway swing gates, sliding driveway gates, and estate entry systems. Each serves a different purpose and carries a different price point based on size, engineering requirements, and material volume.

Pedestrian and garden gates are the most affordable — they're smaller, lighter, and require simpler post footings. Driveway gates scale up significantly because they span 10 to 24 feet and must withstand wind loads, repeated cycling, and vehicle proximity. The choice between swing and sliding depends primarily on your driveway geometry. Swing gates need clearance arc space — if your driveway slopes upward from the street or has limited setback, a sliding gate that travels laterally along the fence line is usually the better engineering choice. Most LA homes with flat driveways and adequate setback use swing gates because they're less mechanically complex and slightly less expensive.

Estate entry gates — the large automated systems with masonry columns, intercom panels, and smart access — represent the premium tier. These are engineered systems, not just gates. The columns alone can cost $3,000–$8,000 depending on material and height, and the automation package adds another $2,000–$5,000 on top of the gate itself.

What Drives the Cost Up (and Down)

Beyond gate type, five factors determine where your project falls within the price range: size, design complexity, material gauge, finish, and automation. Understanding each one gives you real leverage when comparing quotes — and helps you make deliberate trade-offs rather than discovering them after the fact.

Size is straightforward: a wider gate requires more steel, heavier posts, deeper footings, and more labor hours. A 20-foot double swing gate uses roughly twice the material of a 10-foot single — but the engineering is more than twice as complex because the structure must resist greater wind loads and support more weight at the hinge points.

Design complexity has an outsized impact on price. Simple vertical picket designs — the clean, modern look increasingly popular in LA's westside neighborhoods — use standardized components and predictable fabrication time. Ornamental designs with scrollwork, medallions, spear tops, and decorative rosettes require hand-forming, custom jigs, and significantly more welding time. The difference in labor alone can be 60% or more.

Material gauge refers to the wall thickness of the steel tubing. Standard residential gates use 14-gauge steel. Heavier 12-gauge or 10-gauge steel adds cost but provides greater rigidity and longevity — particularly important for larger gates and coastal properties where corrosion resistance matters. For gates under 12 feet, 14-gauge is typically adequate. For estate-scale gates, 12-gauge or heavier is recommended.

Finish options range from basic primer and paint (least expensive, shortest lifespan) to powder coat (the industry standard, $300–$800 add-on) to hot-dip galvanize plus powder coat (the premium choice for coastal LA properties, $800–$1,500 add-on). The finish directly determines how long the gate looks good and resists rust — a critical consideration in LA's salt-air-adjacent neighborhoods from Santa Monica to Pacific Palisades.

Design Complexity

A simple vertical picket gate uses approximately 40% less material and 60% less labor than an ornamental design with scrolls, medallions, and decorative elements. If budget is tight, a clean modern design actually costs significantly less than traditional ornamental iron.

Automation: What It Costs and What You Get

Gate automation transforms a security gate from a physical barrier you have to manually open into a system you actually use every day. The automation conversation has three layers: the opener mechanism, the access control method, and the smart integrations. Each layer adds cost, but each also adds genuine functionality.

The opener mechanism is the core expense. A basic swing arm opener — the most common type for residential swing gates — runs $800–$1,500 installed. Hydraulic underground openers, which hide the mechanism below grade for a cleaner look, cost $1,500–$3,000. Sliding gate motors, which mount at the end of the track, typically run $1,000–$2,500 depending on the gate weight they need to move.

Access control adds the next layer. A basic keypad entry system costs $200–$400. An intercom with camera — allowing you to see and speak with visitors from inside the home — runs $500–$1,500 depending on video quality and integration capability. Smartphone app control, which lets you open the gate from anywhere and receive visitor notifications, adds $300–$800. Battery backup systems, essential for maintaining access during LA's occasional power outages, cost $200–$400.

The total automation package typically adds $1,500–$5,000 to the base gate cost. For most homeowners, the sweet spot is a quality opener with keypad and smartphone control — roughly $1,800–$3,000 all-in. Full estate systems with intercom, camera, battery backup, and smart home integration push toward $4,000–$5,000.

The best security gate is one that's actually closed. A manual gate that homeowners leave open because it's inconvenient to operate provides zero security. Automation pays for itself in actual daily use.

Powder Coat vs. Paint vs. Galvanize

The finish on a security gate isn't cosmetic — it's the primary defense against corrosion, and in Los Angeles, corrosion is a real threat. Salt air from the coast reaches well inland, UV exposure is year-round, and sprinkler overspray creates constant wet-dry cycles on gate surfaces. The wrong finish choice leads to rust within 2–3 years; the right choice keeps a gate looking new for 15–20 years.

Standard paint (brush or spray applied) is the least expensive option but also the least durable. It chips, fades, and requires touch-up every 2–3 years. Most professional fabricators don't recommend it for exterior iron. Powder coat — an electrostatically applied dry finish that's cured in an oven — is the industry standard. It bonds to the metal at a molecular level, resists chipping and fading far better than paint, and typically lasts 10–15 years before needing recoating. The add-on cost is $300–$800 depending on gate size and color.

For properties within five miles of the coast — Santa Monica, Pacific Palisades, Venice, Malibu — the premium option is hot-dip galvanize plus powder coat. The galvanize step submerges the entire gate in molten zinc, creating a sacrificial layer that prevents rust even if the powder coat is scratched. This dual process adds $800–$1,500 but effectively doubles the gate's lifespan in corrosive environments. If you're investing $10,000+ in a coastal security gate, the galvanize upgrade is strongly recommended.

DIY vs. Professional Installation

Security gate installation is not a DIY project, and the reasons go beyond skill level. A security gate is a structural element that must withstand wind loads, repeated mechanical cycling, and in the case of automated gates, electrical and safety code requirements. The engineering matters — and the consequences of getting it wrong range from a gate that sags and binds within months to a gate that fails in wind and damages vehicles or injures people.

Post footings for a driveway gate require excavation to at least 30 inches deep — often 36 inches — with properly mixed concrete and rebar. The footing must be sized to the gate weight and wind load. An undersized footing will shift over time, causing the gate to bind and the automation to fail. Hinge placement must account for the gate's weight distribution and swing arc. Sliding gate tracks must be perfectly level over their entire run — even a quarter-inch deviation causes binding and premature wear on rollers.

For automated gates, electrical work must meet code, and the gate itself must comply with UL 325 safety standards — including entrapment protection sensors and auto-reverse mechanisms. Los Angeles requires permits for gates over 6 feet tall and for any automated gate installation. A professional installer handles the permit process, ensures code compliance, and provides a warranty that covers both materials and labor.

ROI: What a Security Gate Does for Home Value

Security gates typically return 50–80% of their cost in direct home value — a strong ROI compared to most exterior improvements. But the real value often exceeds the appraisal math. In luxury LA neighborhoods like Beverly Hills, Bel Air, and Pacific Palisades, a quality security gate is practically expected. Homes in these areas without gates may actually sell at a discount because buyers factor in the cost of adding one post-purchase. The gate becomes less of an upgrade and more of a baseline expectation.

Beyond resale value, security gates reduce homeowner's insurance premiums by 5–15% on many policies. The insurance savings alone — typically $200–$600 per year — add up to $3,000–$9,000 over a 15-year gate lifespan. Combined with the property value increase, the effective cost of a well-chosen security gate drops significantly below the installation price.

The ROI is strongest when the gate matches the home's architectural style and the neighborhood's expectations. An ornamental iron gate on a modern minimalist home looks out of place — and a cheap-looking gate on a $3 million property actually detracts from value. The best returns come from gates that feel intentional: the right style, the right scale, and the right finish for the specific property and neighborhood.
