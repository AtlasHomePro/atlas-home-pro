Why Your Trees Keep Losing Branches: Common Causes

If you're constantly cleaning up fallen branches — or worse, calling for emergency service after every Santa Ana wind event — the problem isn't just weather. It's almost always a structural issue that proper arboricultural care can address.

Branch failure is the most common tree-related problem on West LA properties. A single large branch falling on a car, pool enclosure, or roof can cause thousands of dollars in damage — and if it happens repeatedly, the root cause is almost never "bad luck." Trees that keep losing branches have identifiable structural problems that, once understood, can usually be corrected through professional arboricultural care.

Cause #1: Weak Branch Unions (Included Bark)

This is the single most common cause of recurring branch failure in mature trees across Beverly Hills, Bel-Air, and the greater West LA area. When two branches grow closely together at a narrow V-angle, bark gets trapped between them instead of forming a strong wood-to-wood connection. This compressed bark — called "included bark" — acts as a wedge that prevents the branches from fusing. The result is a joint that looks solid but has a fraction of the strength of a properly formed union.

Included bark failures are particularly common in:

• Ficus trees — Their rapid growth and dense canopy create numerous competing leaders with included bark at multiple junction points
• Eucalyptus — Co-dominant stems are extremely common and often have extensive included bark
• Chinese elm — Produces many narrow-angle branches that trap bark as they mature
• Jacaranda — The characteristic vase shape develops from competing leaders that often have weak unions

The fix: A qualified arborist can identify included bark unions during a routine assessment. Depending on severity, solutions include structural pruning (reducing weight on the weaker branch), cabling and bracing (installing support hardware to prevent the union from splitting), or selective removal of one competing leader while the tree is still young enough to recover.

Cause #2: Improper Previous Pruning

This is the cause that most surprises homeowners — and it's remarkably common in West LA. Trees that were topped, lion-tailed, or otherwise improperly pruned years ago develop predictable structural problems that lead to recurring branch failure:

Topping damage: When a tree is topped (all major branches cut to stubs), the tree responds by producing dense clusters of sprouts from each cut point. These "watersprouts" grow rapidly but are attached only at the surface of the old stub — they lack the deep structural connection of naturally formed branches. As they grow larger and heavier over years, they break off at the attachment point. A topped tree that was "fixed" 10 years ago is often now dropping branches regularly because those watersprouts have reached a size where their weak attachment can no longer support their weight.

Lion-tailing: Removing all interior branches and foliage, leaving growth only at branch tips. This moves weight to the ends of branches, increasing leverage at attachment points and making branches more susceptible to wind load. Lion-tailed trees sway excessively in wind and experience end-weight failure that drops branches onto whatever is below.

Flush cuts: Cutting branches flush to the trunk (removing the branch collar) prevents proper wound closure, allowing decay to enter the trunk. Over years, this internal decay weakens the attachment of remaining branches at the same junction, creating a chain reaction of failures.

The Topping Cycle

Topping creates a destructive cycle: the tree is topped → it produces weak watersprouts → the sprouts grow large and fail → the homeowner calls to have the "problem branches" removed → the company tops it again → the cycle repeats until the tree is permanently disfigured or must be removed entirely. Break this cycle by hiring an ISA-certified arborist who practices ANSI A300 pruning standards.

Cause #3: Storm and Wind Damage

Los Angeles experiences several types of extreme weather events that cause branch failure:

Santa Ana winds: The primary cause of wind-related tree damage in West LA. These hot, dry offshore winds regularly reach 40–60 mph in foothill and canyon areas, with gusts exceeding 80 mph. Trees that weren't pruned to reduce wind resistance (called "sail reduction" or "crown thinning") are particularly vulnerable. Dense, unpruned canopies catch wind like a sail, and the resulting forces can snap large branches or uproot entire trees.

Winter storms: Pacific storm systems bring sustained winds and heavy rain. Saturated soil reduces root anchorage, and the combination of wind load and weakened root grip causes both branch failure and whole-tree failure. Eucalyptus and Monterey pine are especially vulnerable to storm damage.

Summer branch drop: A phenomenon where apparently healthy branches spontaneously fail on hot, calm summer afternoons. Common in coast live oaks, eucalyptus, and elm. The exact mechanism isn't fully understood, but it's associated with internal moisture stress. Trees with a history of summer branch drop should have at-risk limbs identified and weight-reduced proactively.

Cause #4: Disease and Decay

Several common tree diseases in Los Angeles weaken branches from the inside, causing them to fail under loads they would normally carry:

Heart rot: Fungal pathogens (including Ganoderma, Trametes, and Phellinus species) enter through pruning wounds, storm damage, and natural branch shedding points. They decay the internal heartwood, leaving a shell of sound wood on the outside that eventually becomes too thin to support the branch. A branch with heart rot can look perfectly healthy from the outside — green leaves, intact bark — and fail without warning.

Bacterial wetwood (slime flux): Common in elm, ficus, and oak. Causes internal pressure from bacterial gas production that weakens wood fiber. Visible as dark, wet staining and foul-smelling ooze on the trunk. Branches above active wetwood infections are structurally compromised.

Canker diseases: Localized areas of dead bark caused by fungal or bacterial pathogens. Cankers girdle branches, cutting off water and nutrient flow, leading to branch death and eventual failure. Particularly common in stressed trees — drought, construction damage, or chronic overwatering all predispose trees to canker infections.

A tree that has lost multiple branches in the same area or from the same type of failure point almost certainly has an underlying structural or health issue. The fallen branch is the symptom — the arborist needs to diagnose the cause.

Cause #5: Species-Specific Issues

Some tree species are simply more prone to branch failure than others, regardless of care quality:

Eucalyptus: Evolved to shed branches (cladoptosis) as a survival mechanism in Australian bushfire and drought conditions. This natural branch shedding means eucalyptus will always drop more branches than other species. Regular deadwooding and crown maintenance reduces the risk but doesn't eliminate it entirely.

Monterey pine (Pinus radiata): Fast-growing but structurally weak, with brittle wood that fails under wind load. Common in Pacific Palisades and Malibu-adjacent areas. These trees have a relatively short urban lifespan (60–80 years) and become increasingly hazardous with age.

Brazilian pepper: Produces dense, heavy canopies with brittle wood. Very common in older West LA neighborhoods. Branches snap rather than flex, making them particularly dangerous during wind events.

Ficus: While strong-wooded, the sheer size of mature ficus canopies creates enormous lever forces on branch attachments. Without regular crown thinning, individual branches can weigh thousands of pounds — and when they fail, the damage is proportional.

What to Do If Your Trees Keep Dropping Branches

Stop treating each fallen branch as an isolated incident and address the underlying cause:

1. Get a professional assessment. An ISA-certified arborist will evaluate the entire tree — not just the point where the branch broke — and identify structural defects, disease, included bark, or previous pruning damage that's causing recurring failures.

2. Invest in corrective pruning. If the tree is salvageable, corrective pruning addresses the specific structural issues: removing competing leaders, reducing weight on weak unions, restoring interior branch distribution after lion-tailing, and eliminating watersprouts from previous topping.

3. Consider cabling and bracing. For high-value trees with specific structural weaknesses (included bark at major unions, heavy lateral branches), professional support systems can dramatically reduce failure risk without altering the tree's appearance.

4. Know when to remove. Some trees — particularly those that have been repeatedly topped, have extensive decay, or are species with inherently brittle wood in locations near targets — are better removed and replaced than continuously patched. A good arborist will tell you honestly when a tree has crossed that line.
