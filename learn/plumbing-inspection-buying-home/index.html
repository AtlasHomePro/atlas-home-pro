The Plumbing Inspection Checklist Before Buying an LA Home

A general home inspection gives you a surface read. In LA's pre-1970s housing stock — Beverly Hills, Hancock Park, Brentwood — the plumbing problems that matter most are the ones a general inspector never looks for.

Atlas Home Pro 8 min read April 5, 2026 Licensed & Insured
Licensed & Insured Sewer Camera Inspection Pre-Purchase Reports West LA Specialists

Every real estate transaction in Los Angeles now includes a general home inspection. What it rarely includes is a plumber. The general inspector will run the faucets, flush the toilets, and note any visible water stains. What they will not do is put a camera down your sewer lateral, pressure-test the supply lines, identify galvanized steel from copper by visual inspection alone, or tell you whether the water heater was installed before the Clinton administration. In LA's older housing stock, those are the questions that determine whether you're buying a home or a remediation project.

Why General Inspections Fall Short on Plumbing

A California-licensed general home inspector is trained across a dozen systems simultaneously — roof, foundation, electrical, HVAC, plumbing, and more. Their plumbing section is necessarily a visual survey. They look at what's accessible and exposed. They run water and observe whether it drains. They note evidence of past leaks. What they are not equipped to do is evaluate the condition of supply lines inside walls, the integrity of a sewer lateral 40 feet underground, or the scale buildup inside a 22-year-old water heater.

This gap matters more in Los Angeles than almost anywhere else in California. A significant portion of the housing stock west of the 405 — particularly in Beverly Hills, Hancock Park, Brentwood, and Bel Air — was built between the 1920s and the 1970s. These homes carry plumbing systems installed with materials and methods that have reached or surpassed their expected service life. The problems aren't hypothetical risks; they're actuarial ones.

A pre-purchase plumbing inspection, conducted by a licensed plumber rather than a general inspector, addresses these gaps directly. It typically costs $250–$500 and takes two to three hours on-site. What it surfaces can change the negotiation entirely — or prevent you from closing on a home that will cost $30,000 to remediate in year two.

What a Specialized Plumbing Inspection Covers

A licensed plumber conducting a pre-purchase inspection will move through the home methodically, evaluating systems a general inspector observes only cursorily. The scope typically includes the following:

Sewer Camera Inspection

This is the single most important add-on in any LA home purchase. A flexible fiber-optic camera is fed through the sewer cleanout — typically located at the property line or near the foundation — and pushed through the lateral line to the city main. The plumber watches live video of the pipe's interior, looking for root intrusion, offset joints, bellied sections, clay pipe deterioration, and partial or full blockages. In homes built before 1970, the sewer lateral is almost certainly clay or Orangeburg pipe, both of which fail with age. A sewer camera inspection on a separate line item runs $175–$350; when bundled with a full plumbing inspection it is often included.

Root intrusion is endemic in older Westside neighborhoods. The large trees that give streets like Linden Drive in Beverly Hills and Woodruff Avenue in Brentwood their canopy character are the same trees whose roots seek the moisture gradient around aging clay sewer lines. A lateral with active root intrusion may function acceptably for years — until it doesn't. Replacement of a sewer lateral in West LA typically runs $6,000–$18,000 depending on depth, length, and whether trenchless methods are viable.

Water Pressure Testing

Static water pressure in LA service areas is supposed to fall between 40 and 80 psi. Many older homes exceed this range because pressure-reducing valves were either never installed or have failed without anyone noticing. High pressure — above 80 psi — accelerates wear on supply line connections, washing machine hoses, toilet fill valves, and dishwasher lines. Over time, it causes pinhole leaks in copper pipe. A plumber will attach a gauge to a hose bib and read static pressure in under five minutes. If it's high, a pressure-reducing valve replacement is a $300–$600 fix — or negotiating leverage.

Low pressure tells a different story. Pressure below 40 psi in a home on a municipal water supply can indicate severe mineral scale buildup inside galvanized steel pipes that has reduced the effective bore of the supply lines to a fraction of their original diameter. This is not a repair — it is a repipe.

Supply Line Material Identification

The material your supply pipes are made of matters enormously for a pre-1970s LA home. A plumber will inspect exposed pipe in the garage, under sinks, in crawl spaces, and at utility access points to identify what's running through the walls. The critical materials to identify are galvanized steel, lead, polybutylene, and CPVC — each carries different failure profiles and replacement costs.

Water Heater Age and Condition

The serial number on a water heater encodes its manufacturing date, and a plumber can read it immediately. Water heaters have a functional life of 8–12 years for tank units; many LA homes have units well past this range because they continue to function until they fail catastrophically. A water heater approaching or past the end of its service life is either a negotiating item or a near-term capital expense. Replacement cost for a standard 50-gallon gas water heater in West LA runs $900–$1,600 installed.

The plumber will also inspect the temperature and pressure relief valve, the anode rod condition (if accessible), the flue venting on gas units, and any signs of sediment-related failure at the base.

Drain Flow Testing

Running water and watching it drain is different from actually testing drain flow capacity. A plumber will run multiple fixtures simultaneously, testing whether the drain system can handle realistic load — this reveals partial blockages, undersized drain pipes, and venting problems that don't show up when you run a single faucet during a showing. Slow drains in a pre-1950s home often indicate cast iron drain pipes that have corroded from the inside, reducing capacity. Full-house drain line replacement in a two-story home runs $8,000–$25,000.

Galvanized Steel: The Slow Suffocation

Galvanized steel pipe was the standard supply material for LA homes built through the late 1940s and into the early 1950s. It was coated in zinc to resist corrosion — and it worked, for a while. After 50–70 years, the zinc lining has long since eroded. What remains is bare steel that corrodes from the inside out, building up iron deposits that gradually reduce the pipe's interior bore. A 3/4-inch galvanized supply line in a 1940s home may have an effective bore of 1/4 inch or less. The result is chronically low water pressure that no pressure-reducing valve adjustment can fix, and water that runs discolored after the system sits idle. There is no repair for galvanized steel in this state. The home needs to be repiped.

Whole-home repiping in a 2,500-square-foot West LA home typically runs $8,000–$20,000 depending on access difficulty, number of stories, and the material chosen (copper versus PEX). This is a legitimate price credit to negotiate before closing — or a reason to recalibrate your offer entirely.

Lead Solder and Lead Service Lines

Homes built before 1986 may have lead solder at copper pipe joints; homes built before the 1950s occasionally have lead supply lines, particularly for the service connection between the water main and the home. A plumber can identify lead solder visually and through a scratch test. Lead service lines require a water test, which should be ordered separately. In most West LA neighborhoods served by LADWP, the municipal lines have been updated — but the private side of the service connection remains the homeowner's responsibility. Lead remediation is a health issue as well as a financial one.

Polybutylene: The Silent Recall

Polybutylene pipe was widely installed from roughly 1978 through 1995, particularly in tract homes and some custom builds. It was inexpensive and easy to work with. It also reacts with chlorine disinfectants in municipal water supplies over time, causing the pipe to become brittle and fail — often suddenly, and often inside walls where the leak causes significant damage before it is noticed. There was a class-action settlement in the 1990s, but millions of linear feet of PB pipe were never replaced. In an LA home from this era, confirming whether polybutylene is present is non-negotiable due diligence.

Polybutylene Identification

Polybutylene pipe is gray or blue-gray in color, with a dull surface texture. It connects with plastic or metal crimp fittings rather than soldered joints. If you see gray flexible pipe under sinks, at the water heater, or at the main shutoff in a home built between 1978 and 1995, request a full plumbing inspection before proceeding. Do not rely on visual confirmation alone — a plumber should trace the supply lines through the accessible areas of the home.

CPVC: The Brittleness Problem

Chlorinated polyvinyl chloride (CPVC) was a popular supply material in the 1990s and early 2000s. It handles hot water, it's easy to cut and cement, and it was significantly cheaper than copper at the time. The problem in LA's climate is that CPVC becomes brittle with age, particularly in high-UV exposure areas and where it has been exposed to thermal cycling from the wide temperature swings that characterize LA's summer and winter extremes. CPVC failures are not as insidious as polybutylene — they show up as cracks and joint failures rather than gradual brittleness — but a plumber inspecting a late 1990s or early 2000s home should probe CPVC fittings for signs of stress cracking. Partial CPVC repipe costs depend on scope but can range from $3,000 for a single zone to $12,000 or more for whole-home replacement.

How Plumbing Findings Affect Your Negotiation

A plumbing inspection report is a transactional document as much as a technical one. How you use the findings depends on market conditions, the severity of the issues, and how motivated the seller is — but there are established patterns for how plumbing findings get priced into LA real estate deals.

Price Credits vs. Seller Repairs

In most cases, experienced buyers' agents in the Beverly Hills and Westside markets recommend requesting a price credit rather than asking the seller to repair plumbing issues before closing. The reason is straightforward: a seller under time pressure will do the minimum to satisfy the repair request, and you have no guarantee of the quality of the work. A price credit lets you hire your own contractor after closing and control the scope and quality of the repair. For major items — a sewer lateral replacement, a whole-home repipe — a price credit is almost always the better outcome.

How to Frame the Credit Request

The inspection report from a licensed plumber, combined with two contractor bids for the repair work, gives you the documentation to make a credible credit request. Sellers and their agents are significantly less likely to push back on a credit that is backed by a licensed professional's written findings and multiple repair bids than on a verbal assertion that "the plumbing needs work." The $250–$500 inspection cost produces documentation worth orders of magnitude more at the negotiating table.

A seller who refuses to credit for a failed sewer lateral or galvanized supply lines is telling you something about how they've maintained everything else in the home. Use that information accordingly.

Walk-Away Scenarios

Some plumbing findings are price-reducible. Others warrant walking away entirely — or at minimum, a fundamental reassessment of the deal. If a sewer camera inspection reveals a collapsed Orangeburg sewer lateral with no viable cleanout access, requiring full excavation through a hillside lot in Bel Air, the repair cost and complexity may genuinely exceed the negotiating capacity of the transaction. If galvanized steel supply lines are found alongside lead solder in a pre-1940s home that is priced at market, with a seller who won't move meaningfully on price, the math may not support the purchase at any price within the current offer range.

Timing Your Plumbing Inspection

Schedule your specialized plumbing inspection during the general inspection contingency period — not after. In a competitive LA market, buyers sometimes waive inspection contingencies entirely, which eliminates your ability to use inspection findings for negotiation. If you are purchasing without a contingency, consider having the plumbing inspection completed before your offer goes firm, during any access period the seller has granted. The cost of the inspection is minimal compared to the information value.

What to Ask the Plumber Before You Book

Not every plumber who advertises "home inspections" conducts the same scope of work. Before scheduling a pre-purchase plumbing inspection, confirm that the plumber will perform a sewer camera inspection as part of the service (or as an add-on with a clear price), will provide a written report with photographs suitable for use in a real estate negotiation, holds a current California C-36 (plumbing) contractor license, and can provide repair cost estimates in the same report. The written report is essential — oral findings are not useful at the negotiating table.

Ask specifically about their experience with homes from the era you're purchasing. A plumber who regularly works in Beverly Hills and Hancock Park will recognize the specific failure patterns of 1930s and 1940s-era galvanized systems, the sewer lateral configurations common in those neighborhoods, and the local water authority's standards for service line materials. This local knowledge matters.

In Beverly Hills and Hancock Park, we regularly encounter galvanized supply lines with less than 20% of their original bore remaining. The homeowners had no idea — the flow had degraded so gradually they adapted to it without realizing what they'd lost.

The Cost of Skipping the Inspection

The argument against a specialized plumbing inspection is almost always about time — the contingency period is short, the market is competitive, and adding another appointment feels like friction. This reasoning confuses urgency with importance. The general home inspection takes priority because it covers everything. The plumbing inspection takes priority because plumbing failures are among the most expensive and least visible problems in older LA homes.

The $250–$500 cost of a pre-purchase plumbing inspection is among the lowest-risk expenditures in a transaction where the purchase price may be $2 million or more. If the inspection surfaces nothing significant, you close with additional confidence. If it surfaces a $15,000 sewer lateral replacement, you have documentation for a credit request — or the information to walk away. If you skip it and discover the problem after closing, you own it entirely.

In pre-1970s LA homes particularly, the probability that a specialized inspection finds something significant is not hypothetical. It is the base case, not the exception. The question is not whether to have the inspection — it is whether you want to know before or after.
