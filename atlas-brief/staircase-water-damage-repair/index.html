The Staircase Line Item No Broker Shows You.

A broker's expense sheet will never line-item rotten stair treads. On one of our buildings, just under half of the exterior treads were soft from water damage. This is what the repair actually cost us, what a specialist would have charged, and why I did not pour concrete.

David SafaiEditor · Publisher
PublishedApril 21, 2026
Read time6 minutes
DatelineLos Angeles, CA
Four takeaways Exterior stairs are a line item you inherit, not one you see on the flyer.
  1. Close to 50% of treads needed replacement. You do not see this in a broker's T-12 or in an operating statement. You see it when you walk the staircase with a screwdriver.
  2. In-house Atlas cost: ~$1,500 per staircase. Roughly $500 in materials (treads, screws, paint, primer) and $1,000 in labor with our own crew.
  3. Specialist pricing: $2,500 to $3,000. For comparable scope. The delta is margin and mobilization, not quality.
  4. Concrete is the fifty-year answer, not always the right one. A concrete stair replacement requires a structural engineer to sign off on the existing stringers and foundation carrying the new dead load. We passed that cost this round.

The first time I walked these stairs, a few of them gave a little under weight. Not the whole step, just the center of the board, the way an old diving board flexes. That is water, sitting between the tread and the stringer for a long time, doing what water does to softwood. The top coat of paint hides it. Every owner I have bought a building from has sent me an expense document that did not mention this. That is not deceit. That is the document not asking the right question.

So we asked. We went tread by tread, pushed a screwdriver into the seam where the tread meets the nosing, and marked the soft ones with chalk. By the time we were done, just under half of the treads on the run were going to have to come out. Not a catastrophe. Not a structural failure. A normal line item on an older exterior wood stair.

The things that blow a pro forma are not the things on the pro forma. , Atlas field note, April 2026

The inspection

Here is the part that most operators skip. You walk the stairs in the rain, or the morning after. You look at the underside of each tread, the stringer where it meets the side of the building, and the landing where water pools. Wherever paint is bubbling or the nosing is dark, that tread is probably done. The treads that look worst from above are not always the worst ones, water damage comes from below.

On this run we had two kinds of failure. First, classic rot in the center of the tread where water pooled against a slightly warped board. Second, rot at the stringer connection where the fasteners were seated just low enough to hold standing water after a storm. The second kind is the dangerous one, because it shortens the bearing surface and you do not see it until the screws pop.

A specialist will come in at $2,500 on the low end, $3,000 on the higher end for comparable scope, and they will be using the same lumber from the same yard. The premium is mobilization, margin, and a warranty, which has a real value if you are not doing the work yourself. We are, so we pocket the delta and reinvest it into the next repair.

The concrete question

The other path, the one a lot of operators ask about, is to rip the whole wood staircase out and replace it with poured concrete. Concrete stairs in LA multifamily are a fifty-year asset. You do not re-paint them. You do not re-tread them. You hose them down and move on. On paper that is the right answer.

In practice it is not always the right answer, and here is why. Every time you change what is carrying a load on a building, a structural engineer has to sign off on whether the existing stringers, footings, and attachment to the building can carry the new dead load. Wet concrete is heavy, and cured concrete stays heavy. On a 1960s or 1970s multifamily, the original stair was framed to carry a wood stair. Converting it to concrete without an engineer's letter is a liability you do not want.

The engineer is $1,500 to $3,000. The pour, the formwork, the rebar, the demo of the existing stair, call it another $10K to $20K depending on the run. By the time you are done, a concrete replacement on one run is $12K on the low end and $25K or more for a long run with a landing. For a fifty-year asset this is fine. For a building I bought last year where I am still figuring out which walls leak, I did not want to write that check this season. We repaired the wood, documented everything, and put concrete on the list for the next capital cycle.

Why this is not optional, SB 721 and SB 326

For California multifamily owners, this work is not cosmetic, it is compliance. SB 721, enacted in 2018, requires exterior elevated elements on buildings of three or more residential units, that means decks, balconies, stairways, walkways, and their railings and supports, to be inspected by a qualified professional every six years. The first inspection cycle ran to January 1, 2026. If you own multifamily in LA and you have not had your inspection done, or you are buying a building, this is on you.

SB 326 is the HOA version. Condominium associations with three or more units were required to inspect exterior elevated elements by January 1, 2025 and every nine years after. It is the same spirit, different structure.

Here is what matters for a buyer. You can walk a building on market, ask the seller for the SB 721 inspection report, and learn in one document whether the exterior stairs, landings, and balconies have been tagged. If the report exists and is clean, you are in good shape. If the report does not exist, or it exists and has open items, you now have a real number to deduct from the offer. A tagged balcony repair runs $4K to $12K depending on the waterproofing scope. A tagged stair run, what we just did, runs the $1,500 to $3,000 we wrote down above, per run.

Owner's Note If you are under contract on a pre-1978 LA multifamily and the seller cannot produce an SB 721 inspection, make it a due-diligence item. It is cheaper to find out before close than after. A qualified inspector in LA runs $500, $1,500 for a report on a small to mid-size building, and the report itself is a negotiating instrument.
