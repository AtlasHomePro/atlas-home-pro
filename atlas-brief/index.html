Market Commentary · Cover Story

The LA Cost Ceiling Didn't Break. It Just Moved.

Hard-cost inflation in multifamily has not reverted. It has rotated — from lumber and metals into labor, soft costs, and the slow tax of municipal delay. Five active jobs, five honest invoices, one conclusion the trade press is missing.

By David Safai · Editor & Publisher · Apr. 9, 2026 · 14 min read

For two years, the construction trade press has been quietly writing a eulogy for the 2021 cost spike. Lumber came off its peak. Metals softened. Permit volume slowed enough that subcontractors, for the first time in a decade, started returning phone calls on the same day.

The story was that costs had finally broken. On five of our own active jobs, the story is that costs never broke — they rotated. What came out of structural framing went into labor. What came out of cladding went into mechanical systems and the opaque, compounding cost of municipal delay.

The ceiling on Los Angeles multifamily hard costs, in other words, didn't fall. It moved from a place we could see on a material invoice to a place that lives, inconveniently, inside a project schedule. We'll argue in this piece that schedule is now the most expensive line item on a multifamily pro forma — and almost nobody is underwriting it correctly.

Consider a forty-unit Type-V wood-frame job, entitled and permitted in the mid-Wilshire district last year. The lumber package delivered at 7% below its 2022 quote. The structural steel was flat. Yet the all-in cost per gross square foot finished 11% above pro forma, and that delta was not an accounting error.

The delta lived in three places, each of which is now structural to the LA market: extended conditions on a longer-than-promised plan-check, labor rates that climbed despite a softening general economy, and an insurance premium repriced twice between permit issuance and Certificate of Occupancy. None of those lines show up on a steel-and-lumber index.
