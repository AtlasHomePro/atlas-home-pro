PG&E / LADWP Meter Set: The Two-Week Wait Nobody Budgets For.

Fig. 08-A — Utility coordination log, Felix project · Meter set request filed Mar 28
Meter Set — Status Log
Request filedMar 28, 2026
Confirmed dateApr 11, 2026
Elapsed (req → set)14 cal. days
Schedule impact+9 days TCO slip
Carrying cost~$4,100
Projects affected2 of 4 active

Submitted a dual-service meter set request for The Felix on Fairfax on March 28. The LADWP queue returned a date of April 11 — fourteen calendar days, against the five-day window our GC had baked into the schedule. The difference is $4,100 in carrying cost at current financing and nine days of TCO slip.

This is not a new problem. It is a permanently underestimated one. The soft-cost pro forma on every project in our pipeline now includes an explicit utility-coordination buffer of twelve business days for meter sets, regardless of what the utility website says the queue is. We learned this on Olympic Towers in Q3 2025 and apparently needed to relearn it.

Insurance Renewal, 2026: The Quote We Got, the Number We Expected, and the Gap.

Renewal cycle opened March 15. By the 31st, three carriers had passed entirely on the older Class-C stock in the portfolio; one came back at 34% above 2025 premium; one came in at 27% above. The sprinklered asset renewed at +8% — consistent with what peers are reporting.

The underwriting logic is now explicit where it was previously implicit: carriers are pricing elevation, ignition-source proximity, and exterior cladding type as primary variables. A wood-frame, non-sprinklered three-story built before 1980 in a brush-adjacent zip is priced as a near-uninsurable asset at some carriers' current rate sheets. We have one such building. We are holding it, not trading it, and the insurance math is a central reason why: a sale at current carrying cost versus cap rate is disadvantageous; the ULA hit alone is a six-figure haircut.

Note to file: the soft-story seismic retrofit on the Hollywood Dell building (see Entry 005) will be a meaningful insurance-reduction conversation at the next renewal cycle. Need to get the completion certification in hand before September 15 policy date.

Under Review

ED-1 Parcel, West Adams: Under Review at the City. What We're Watching.

Our 7,200-square-foot C2 parcel on West Adams submitted under Executive Directive 1 on January 9. We are eleven weeks in as of this entry. The housing element compliance window that makes this parcel viable closes if the city's rezoning calendar moves, which it has once already. We are monitoring the LADBS case portal daily.

The ED-1 pathway bought us one thing that the standard discretionary process could not: an administrative completeness determination in 30 days rather than six months. We had it. The plan-check queue, however, is no different whether you enter through the front door or the ministerial one. At 147 days average per current data, we are projecting an April groundbreaking as optimistic and June as the operating case.

Pro forma note: land carry at current rate adds $1,200/month per unit to the effective cost basis for every month past the April target. We have seven units in the proposed program. The pencil still works at June. It starts to struggle at September.

The Hollywood Dell soft-story retrofit is in final phase. Steel moment-frame installation at the parking-level soft story completed February 20. Final framing, shear-wall sheathing, and fireproofing are scheduled through April 30. We are approximately five months past the city's October 1 compliance deadline — late, but not in violation (permit was issued in November, and LADBS has been tracking compliance by permit issuance, not completion date, for properties that submitted on time).

Total project cost is tracking at $148,000, against an original budget of $131,000. The delta lives in a single line: the structural engineer required two additional hold-down brackets after the first-round inspection. The change order was $17,400. There is no appealing structural field observations.

LADBS Plan-Check, Olympic Towers Reclad: 147 Days and Counting.

Submitted the Olympic Towers exterior reclad plans on October 14, 2025. As of February 18 — 127 days in — we are still in the plan-check queue at LADBS Van Nuys. The current posted average is 147 days. We are therefore four days ahead of average, which is not a statement of pride.

The scope is a re-roof and exterior cladding replacement on a 32-unit 1968 construction. It is not a complex plan set. The plan-check backlog is not a function of complexity; it is a function of staffing. LADBS hired fourteen plan-check engineers in Q4 2025 against a posted demand of forty. The gap is visible on every project in our pipeline and in the portfolios of every operator we talk to.

Carrying cost note: the scaffolding we mobilized in anticipation of a December permit date has been standing since November. At $2,100/month rental plus insurance rider, the delay has cost $8,400 in scaffolding holding cost alone, before any consideration of postponed rent-roll capture from unoccupied units we deferred filling pending the construction.

Entitlement

Hillside Entitlement, Beachwood Canyon: The Geology Report Changed the Program.

The preliminary geotechnical report on the Beachwood Canyon hillside parcel came back January 28. Expansive soils across 60% of the buildable footprint. The original program — a three-unit SB9 lot split with two detached ADUs — is not feasible on the current grading plan without a retaining-wall system the structural engineer priced at $88,000. That number changes the pro forma.

We are revising the program to a single primary structure with one attached ADU, which reduces the retaining-wall scope to $31,000 and allows us to preserve the program's internal rate of return above our minimum threshold. The entitlement application will be refiled in March with the revised site plan. The six-week reset is an unpleasant number; the alternative is forcing a program that doesn't pencil through a $200,000 grading solution.

Note on hillside due diligence generally: a preliminary geotech report ($2,400–$4,800 on most LA hillside sites) before any design work is now a non-negotiable line in our pre-acquisition checklist. This is the second time in three years we have had a program redesigned by a soils report. There will not be a third.

Six months into lease-up at Olympic Towers. Twenty-one of twenty-four units leased; three remain, all on the upper floor with the larger floor plan. Average effective rent of $2,840/month is 3.2% above pro forma — a number I do not celebrate, because it reflects demand conditions more than underwriting precision.

The two concessions granted (one month free on units 14 and 22) were to move units that sat past 30 days with consistent showings but no conversion. The asking rents on those two units were on the high end of our own range; the market agreed. We adjusted. Both units leased within a week of the concession offer. The lesson is not that concessions work; it is that holding an asking rent past your own absorption model for sixty days is expensive in ways that don't show up in rent roll — they show up in operating cost and schedule.

December 29, 2025 The Felix on Fairfax — Fairfax District, L.A. The Felix on Fairfax
Year 4 — Ops

The Felix on Fairfax, Year Four: What the Pro Forma Knew and What It Didn't.

Year four is when a pro forma's fiction becomes obvious. The rent schedule held; the maintenance budget did not. The Felix — 43 units, Type-V, Fairfax District, delivered Q1 2022 — turned in a year-four NOI that was 6.1% below the acquisition model. The revenue line was within 1.4% of forecast. The expense line was 14.2% above it.

The delta is not a mystery. It is four line items: HVAC service contracts renegotiated at year-two market rates (+22%); landscaping contract repriced by the vendor at renewal (+18%); a plumbing repair on the third-floor stack that the building inspection four years ago did not flag ($14,800 in Q2 2025); and the insurance renewal described above in Entry 007. None of these were emergencies. All of them were predictable with a longer observation window than a pro forma's five-year model provides.

The first-principles lesson, which we have written about elsewhere: a pro forma is a planning document, not a prediction. The value of holding a building through year four is that you replace forecast with fact. The fact is that maintenance on a four-year-old Type-V wood-frame building in Los Angeles is 28–32% more expensive annually than the model assumes. We are writing that number into every new pro forma on the desk today.
