Step 01 · 15 Minutes
Contact
Win before you walk in.
The Simple Version
Call 30 minutes out. Build rapport on the phone. Show up looking like a pro.
Why This Step Matters
The sale starts before you ring the doorbell. A warm call 30 min before arrival sets the tone — it's the difference between a customer who's ready to trust you and one who's guarded. First impressions start on the phone, not at the door.
What to Say & Do
30-Min Pre-Arrival Call
"Hey [Name], this is [Your Name] from Atlas Home Pro. Just calling to let you know I'm about 30 minutes away. I'm really looking forward to helping you out today — can you confirm the best door to come to?"
Truck Check (Before You Step Out)
Shirt tucked. Clean hands. Breath spray. No sunglasses on your head. Look in the mirror. You are the expert — look like one. Park on the street, never in the driveway.
🔑
Memorize This Line
"I'm looking forward to helping you out today."
Common Mistakes — Don't Do These
✗Don't call and just say "I'm on my way" — use the call to BUILD rapport, not just notify
✗Don't park in the driveway — it blocks the owner's access and starts the visit wrong
✗Don't walk up with coffee in your hand or sunglasses on your head
🎬 Role Play Exercise
Partner A plays the customer answering the pre-arrival call. Partner B is the tech. Record it. Play it back. Did it feel warm and genuine or robotic and transactional? Run it 3 times. Switch roles.
🚪
Step 02 · 15 Minutes
Arrival
Confidence + Humility = Trust.
The Simple Version
Park on the street. Walk up confident. Make them feel smart for calling you.
Why This Step Matters
The first 60 seconds at the door are worth more than 60 minutes of selling inside. The customer is judging everything. They need to feel they made the right call hiring Atlas — before you've touched a single tool.
What to Say & Do
At the Door
"Hi [Name], I'm [Your Name] from Atlas Home Pro. It's great to meet you. [Hand business card.] I've got your work order right here — before I grab my tools, do you mind if I ask you a few quick questions so I can make sure I take care of everything for you today?"
Body Language
Firm handshake. Eye contact. Smile. Don't look around the house or let your eyes wander. Your entire focus is on this person. Confident and humble is the combination — not arrogant.
🔑
Memorize This Line
"I want to make sure I take care of EVERYTHING for you today."
Common Mistakes — Don't Do These
✗Don't say anything that makes the customer feel dumb for not knowing — ever
✗Don't go straight to the problem without connecting with the person first
✗Don't be arrogant — confidence without humility kills trust instantly
🎬 Role Play Exercise
Walk up to a door, knock, and deliver your arrival intro. Partner plays a skeptical homeowner who has had bad experiences before. Film it. Watch your body language. Did you seem approachable? Did you smile naturally?
📋
Step 03 · 15 Minutes
Assessment
Ask first. Diagnose second. Never assume.
The Simple Version
Ask 3 questions before touching anything. Listen more than you talk. Your toolbox stays closed.
Why This Step Matters
Customers don't buy fixes — they buy someone who understands their situation. Questions build trust faster than any sales tactic. Silence after a question is golden — let them fill it. You learn more and they feel heard.
The 3 Questions — Ask Every Time
Question 1 — Rapport
"This is a beautiful home — how long have you lived here?"
This is rapport, not small talk. A 20-year homeowner cares differently than a 2-year owner. You learn their attachment to the property.
Question 2 — The Problem
"Tell me exactly what's been happening — walk me through it."
Let them talk. Don't interrupt. Take notes. The more they talk, the more they trust you.
Question 3 — History
"Has this happened before, or is this the first time?"
This tells you if it's a symptom of something bigger — and sets up the checklist inspection naturally.
🔑
Memorize This Line
"Tell me exactly what's been happening — walk me through it."
Common Mistakes — Don't Do These
✗Don't start diagnosing while they're still talking — wait until they fully finish
✗Don't ask questions and then answer them yourself
✗Don't open your toolbox before you've asked all 3 questions
🎬 Role Play Exercise
Partner plays a homeowner with a leaky pipe under the kitchen sink. Tech must ask only questions for 3 full minutes before suggesting anything — no diagnosing, no solutions. Just questions and listening. Switch roles and debrief.
🔧
Step 04 · 15 Minutes
Pitch
Fix what they called for. Then look for more.
The Simple Version
Solve the minimum first. Earn trust. Then and only then look for additional issues.
Why This Step Matters
If you go straight for upsells before fixing the reason they called, you look like a salesman, not an expert. Fix what they called for. Do it right. Now they trust you. Now they'll listen to everything else you found.
What to Say & Do
Present the Minimum Fix First
"Based on what you've told me and what I'm seeing, here's what's causing your issue today..." [Explain clearly in plain English. Zero jargon. Speak like a smart friend, not a textbook.]
Transition to the Inspection
"While I'm here, I always like to do a quick check of everything related so we don't have to come back out. Would that be okay with you?"
Always get a yes before you start inspecting. Never expand scope without permission.
🔑
Memorize This Line
"While I'm here, I always like to do a quick check — would that be okay?"
Common Mistakes — Don't Do These
✗Don't push upsells before fixing the reason they called — you'll lose trust immediately
✗Don't use technical jargon — speak like a knowledgeable friend, not an instruction manual
✗Don't expand scope without asking — "While I'm here" is not permission
🎬 Role Play Exercise
Present the minimum fix on a plumbing issue without mentioning anything else. Then transition naturally into "while I'm here." Partner tries to say no to the inspection. Tech must respond without pressure — why it benefits the customer, not the company.
✅
Step 05 · 20 Minutes
Checklist Inspection
Same process every time. No exceptions.
The Simple Version
Run your checklist. Pass/fail every item. Take photos. Walk the customer through failures in plain English.
Why This Step Matters
The checklist does two things: it finds real problems that generate revenue, and it proves to the customer you are thorough. Photos are everything — they remove doubt and justify the price. A tech who skips the checklist is leaving money on the table every single job.
What to Say & Do
During Inspection
Work through your printed checklist item by item. Mark each pass or fail. Photograph every fail with your phone before touching anything. The photo is your documentation AND your sales tool.
Presenting a Failed Item
"I want to show you what I found. This right here [show photo on your phone] — this is your [item name]. What's happening is [plain English explanation]. It's not an emergency today, but here's what happens if we leave it..."
Framing Urgency — Honestly
"If there's a safety concern, I'll tell you clearly. If it's something that can wait, I'll tell you that too. I want to be completely honest with you."
🔑
Memorize This Line
"I want to show you what I found — here's the photo."
Common Mistakes — Don't Do These
✗Don't skip the checklist because you're busy — it's the primary revenue generator
✗Don't show photos without explaining what the customer is looking at in plain language
✗Don't manufacture urgency — real problems sell themselves. Fake urgency destroys trust.
🎬 Role Play Exercise
Partner hides 3 "problems" in a mock setup. Tech must find them using only the checklist, photograph them, then explain each one to a 10-year-old in plain English. Time it — should take under 8 minutes per problem.
📊
Step 06 · 20 Minutes
Package Pricing
Good. Better. Best. Always present all three.
The Simple Version
Present 3 options in writing. Start with the best. Walk down. Let them choose.
Why This Step Matters
People buy on emotion and justify with logic. Three options give the customer control — they feel like they're choosing, not being sold. Starting with the best anchors their expectations high. Most customers land in the middle. One price = a yes or no. Three prices = which one.
What to Say & Do
Setup Line
"Based on everything I found today, I put together three options for you. I always like to show you all three so you can decide what makes the most sense for your home and budget. Okay?"
Present Best Option First (Gold)
"This is our Atlas Complete solution — it addresses the immediate issue plus everything I found today, with a 2-year warranty on all parts and labor. This is what I would do if it were my home. The investment is $[X]."
Present Middle Option (Silver)
"This is our Atlas Standard — it fixes the main issue plus the two most critical items I found. One-year warranty. Investment is $[X]."
Present Minimum (Bronze)
"This is the minimum repair for what you called about today. Gets you back up and running. Investment is $[X]. I want to be honest though — the other items I found will still need attention."
🔑
Memorize This Line
"This is what I would do if it were my home."
Common Mistakes — Don't Do These
✗Don't present only one price — you'll lose on price every single time
✗Don't start with the cheap option — always anchor high first
✗Don't rush through the presentation — let each option land before moving to the next
✗Don't apologize for any price — present it confidently or the customer won't trust the value
🎬 Role Play Exercise
Present all three written options to a partner. Partner plays a price-conscious customer who immediately challenges the Gold option price. Tech must present without apologizing for any price and without immediately dropping to the cheap option. Run 5 rounds.
📲
Step 07 · 15 Minutes
Backup
Stuck? Call reinforcements. Zero ego.
The Simple Version
If the customer pushes back hard or you can't close — call your manager. Right now. No shame in it.
Why This Step Matters
The manager call is one of the most powerful tools in sales. It's not weakness — it's strategy. The customer hears that the company stands behind the tech. It changes the entire dynamic. A fresh voice often closes what a great presentation couldn't.
When to Trigger the Backup Call
Triggers
Customer says: "That's too much." / "I need to think about it." / "I want to get other quotes." / Customer seems frustrated or upset. → Pick up the phone. Don't try to muscle through alone.
How to Set Up the Call
"You know what, let me do this — I want to make sure we take care of you right. Let me get my manager on the phone for just a second. He loves to talk to customers directly when I have someone this important." [Call manager. Hand phone to customer.]
If Offering a Discount
"I might be able to work something out for you, but I'd need something in return. If we do that, would you be willing to leave us a 5-star Google review? That's how Atlas grows — one happy customer at a time."
🔑
Memorize This Line
"Let me get my manager on the phone — he loves talking to customers directly."
Common Mistakes — Don't Do These
✗Don't drop the price without asking for something in return — a review, a referral, or a yard sign
✗Don't argue with a frustrated customer — that's what backup is for
✗Don't let ego stop you from picking up the phone — pride costs you the sale
🎬 Role Play Exercise
Customer says "I want to get 3 quotes first." Tech must use the backup call strategy. Manager (second partner) must get on the phone and close. Debrief: what did the manager say that the tech couldn't? What's transferable?
🤝
Step 08 · 20 Minutes
Close the Sale
Ask for the business. Then shut up.
The Simple Version
Use Tommy's exact closing line. Then say nothing. The next person to talk loses.
Why This Step Matters
Most techs do everything right and then don't ask for the sale. You MUST ask. And then you MUST be quiet. Silence is pressure. The customer will fill it — and most of the time, they'll say yes. The tech who keeps talking after asking gives the customer an easy out.
What to Say & Do
The One Close — Use It Every Time
"[Name], what is it going to take to earn your business today? The reason I ask is — Atlas has grown over 40% this year because of referrals. Once we do this for you, I'm sure you're going to tell your friends and neighbors about us."
→ STOP TALKING. Do not fill the silence. Count to 15 in your head if you have to. The next person to speak loses.
After Yes
"Perfect. Let me get the paperwork started. We're going to take great care of you."
Secure the Relationship — Before You Leave
After the job: walk the customer through everything you did. Explain all warranties. Leave the space cleaner than when you arrived. Ask: "Do you have any questions about anything we did today?" Then: ask for the Google review out loud. Don't just send a text — ask in person.
🔑
The Only Close You Need
"What is it going to take to earn your business today?"
THEN SHUT UP.
Common Mistakes — Don't Do These
✗Don't ask and then keep talking — silence is the close, not more selling
✗Don't leave without walking through all completed work — buyer's remorse kills reviews
✗Don't forget to ask for the Google review before you walk out
✗Don't leave if the space is messier than when you arrived — presentation is part of the 5-star review
🎬 Role Play Exercise
Deliver the full close line, then count to 15 seconds in your head. Do not speak. Partner must resist saying yes for as long as possible. Debrief: who cracked first? How did the silence feel? Run it 5 times until silence feels natural, not uncomfortable.
Module 9 — Memorize These
Objection Handling

These are the 5 objections your techs will hear every single day. The first objection is almost never the real one — always ask a follow-up question to find what's actually behind it.

Golden Rule: The first objection is almost never the real objection. Ask a follow-up question first. Find the underlying concern. Then handle it.
"That's too expensive."
Your Response
"I completely understand. Can I ask — too expensive compared to what? Because what I want to make sure is that you understand exactly what you're getting. This isn't just a repair, it's a [X]-year warranty, done right the first time. What part of the price is the biggest concern?"
"I need to think about it."
Your Response
"Totally fair. Can I ask what specifically you want to think through? Because I'm right here — I'd rather answer any questions now than have you make a decision without all the information."
"I want to get other quotes."
Your Response
"That's smart — you should always know your options. Let me get my manager on the phone real quick. He can answer any questions and I want to make sure you feel completely confident before I leave."
"My spouse needs to decide."
Your Response
"No problem at all. Is your spouse available right now? I can wait, or we can do a quick call together. I'd hate for them to make this decision without seeing what I found today."
"Can you just give me a price over the phone?"
Your Response
"I wish I could give you an accurate number without seeing it — but honestly if I quote you without looking, I might be way off and waste your time. What I can do is get out there fast and give you an exact number with no obligation. When works best?"
🎬 Objection Role Play
Partner A delivers all 5 objections in random order. Partner B responds from memory — no reading. Run 3 rounds. Switch. Film it. Goal: responses feel natural and conversational, not scripted. Time: 30 minutes total.
